Strategy Testing

Trading strategies are just rules that decide when to buy and sell. But how do you know if those rules actually work? You test them — carefully, in stages — before risking real money. Here's the process:

Example — SMA Crossover on Gold

This strategy watches two moving averages of the gold price — a "fast" one (say, the last 10 prices) and a "slow" one (say, the last 30 prices). When the fast average crosses above the slow one, it means prices are picking up momentum → buy. When it crosses below → sell. The numbers 10 and 30 are the "settings" you'll tune in the steps below.

  • 1. Backtest

    Replay past price data and pretend you were trading with your strategy. Did it make money? How much did it lose at its worst? How often did it win? Think of it like watching a recording of a football match and seeing how your game plan would have played out. This is your first sanity check — if it doesn't work on old data, it won't work on new data either.

    e.g. "Run SMA Crossover with fast=10, slow=30 on last week's gold prices → it made $420 with a 58% win rate. Okay, the idea has legs."

  • 2. Parameter Sweep

    Every strategy has settings you can tweak (like how sensitive it is to price changes). A sweep tests every combination of settings and ranks the results — so instead of guessing, you find out which settings actually performed best. It's like trying every dial position on a radio to find the clearest signal.

    e.g. "Try fast=5,10,15,20 and slow=20,30,40,50 — that's 16 combinations. The sweep tells you fast=15, slow=40 scored best."

  • 3. Walk-Forward Validation

    Here's the catch: the "best" settings from a sweep might just be lucky — they fit the past perfectly but fall apart on new data (like memorizing answers to a specific test instead of actually learning the subject). Walk-forward solves this by repeatedly finding the best settings on one chunk of data, then testing them on the next chunk they've never seen. If performance holds up across multiple unseen chunks, the strategy is genuinely robust — not just a fluke. If it fails here, go back to step 2 and try different ranges.

    e.g. "Week 1 data picks fast=15, slow=40 as best → test on Week 2 (unseen) → still profitable? Week 3 picks best → test on Week 4 → still works? Great, you can trust these settings."
